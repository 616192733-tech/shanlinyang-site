Clothing Sample Approval Process: How to Review & Approve Samples from China

PUBLISHED 2026-06-29 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~14 MIN READ

One of the most exciting moments for any fashion entrepreneur is receiving that first package from a manufacturer in China. You open the box, eager to see your vision brought to life. However, this is also one of the most critical phases in the manufacturing journey. The **clothing sample approval process China** is not just about looking at a garment and saying "I like it." It is a technical, multi-stage verification process designed to catch errors before they become permanent in bulk production.

Skipping steps or rushing through sample approval is the number one cause of production failure. A small mistake on a single sample—like a slightly tight armhole or a zipper that doesn't quite match the fabric—becomes a disaster when multiplied across a 500-unit order. At Shanlinyang Apparel, we guide our clients through a rigorous approval protocol. In this guide, we will walk you through the types of samples you need, how to review them like a professional, and how to give the final "green light" for bulk manufacturing.

1. Understanding the Different Types of Samples in Garment Production

In the world of professional fashion manufacturing, there isn't just "one sample." Different stages of the process require different types of physical references. Understanding these terms is essential for communicating with your clothing sample approval process China partner:

Proto Sample (Development Sample): This is the very first version of your design. Its purpose is to check the design concept, proportions, and basic construction. It is often made using "available" fabric (similar in weight but maybe the wrong color) to save time and cost. If the pockets look too big or the neckline is too deep, you fix it here.

Fit Sample: Once the design is locked in, you need to check the fit. This sample must be made in the correct size (usually your base size, like Medium) and on the actual fabric you intend to use for production. Fit samples are often worn by a fit model or placed on a mannequin to ensure the garment moves and drapes correctly on a human body.

Size Set: Before bulk cutting begins, many brands request a "size set." This involves making one sample in every size you plan to sell (e.g., XS, S, M, L, XL). This ensures that your "grading" (how the pattern scales up and down) is correct and that the proportions remain flattering across all sizes.

Pre-Production Sample (PPS): This is the most important sample of all. The PPS is a 100% accurate representation of what will come off the production line. It must use the final bulk fabric, final trims, final labels, and be made by the actual production line (not a sampling room). Once you approve the PPS, the factory can "press the button" on bulk production.

TOP Sample (Top of Production): This is a sample pulled directly from the first few pieces of the actual bulk production run. It is sent to the brand to confirm that the production line is following the approved PPS perfectly. It's the final "checkpoint" before the goods are packed and shipped.

2. The Golden Rule: Why You Must Never Skip the Proto Sample Phase

New brand owners are often in a rush to get to market. They might say, "I trust the factory, let's just go straight to PPS and start production." This is a dangerous mistake. The clothing sample approval process China relies on iterative improvement. Here is why the Proto phase is non-negotiable:

Visualizing the 2D Tech Pack: Even the best tech pack is still a 2D document. A physical Proto sample shows you how those 2D lines translate into 3D space. You might realize that a seam you thought would be "hidden" is actually very visible, or that a particular fabric is too stiff for the intended silhouette.

Identifying Construction Challenges: Sometimes, a design that looks great on paper is actually very difficult to sew efficiently. The Proto stage allows the manufacturer to identify "bottlenecks" in construction. They might suggest changing a seam type to improve durability or reduce labor costs. If you skip this, these issues will haunt you during bulk production.

Testing Fabric Behavior: Not all fabrics behave the same way under a needle. A Proto sample helps you see if the fabric "puckers" at the seams or if it loses its shape after being handled. It's much cheaper to change your fabric choice at the Proto stage than after you've purchased 500 meters for bulk production.

3. How to Conduct a Professional Fit Review and Measurement Check

When your sample arrives, you must put on your "Quality Control" hat. A professional clothing sample approval process China review involves more than just trying it on. You need to be methodical:

The Measurement Check: Lay the garment flat on a large table. Use a professional garment measuring tape (flexible, not metal). Refer to your Tech Pack's "Measurement Spec" and measure every "Point of Measure" (POM). Record the actual measurement next to the target measurement. If the tech pack says 50cm and the garment is 51cm, it might be within "tolerance," but if it's 53cm, it's a fail.

The Fit Session: If possible, have a fit model try on the garment. Ask them to move—sit down, raise their arms, walk around. Look for "tension lines" (wrinkles that point to where the garment is too tight) or "sagging" (where it's too loose). Check the balance: Does the hemline sit level? Do the side seams stay straight or do they twist toward the front or back?

Checking the "Hand-Feel" and Finishing: Feel the fabric. Is it as soft as you expected? Check the "inside" of the garment. Are the seams clean? Are there loose threads? Test the trims—zip the zippers multiple times, snap the buttons. Everything should feel durable and high-quality.

4. Communicating Feedback: Using Revision Comments and Photos Effectively

The biggest hurdle in the clothing sample approval process China is the language and cultural barrier. To get the results you want, your feedback must be visual and precise. Never just say "it's too big."

Annotated Photos are Essential: Take high-quality photos of the sample on the model or mannequin. Use arrows and text overlays to point out exactly where the problem is. For example, draw a circle around a gaping neckline and write "Reduce neck opening by 2cm."

Use a Comment Sheet: Create a simple document that lists every revision required. Categorize them into "Fit," "Construction," "Fabric/Trims," and "Branding." This allows the manufacturer's pattern maker to systematically address every point without missing anything.

Side-by-Side Comparisons: If you are on your second round of sampling, show the "New Sample" next to the "Old Sample" or the "Tech Pack." This helps the factory see that they followed (or missed) your previous instructions. Clarity is the enemy of production errors.

5. The Final Approval: Transitioning from PPS to Bulk Production

Once you are happy with a sample, it's time for the "Pre-Production Sample" (PPS). This is the "Golden Sample." The clothing sample approval process China concludes here. Once you sign off on the PPS, you are essentially saying: "Make the next 500 pieces exactly like this one."

The "Sealed" Sample: Once you approve a PPS, you should keep one copy at your office and the factory should "seal" (keep) another copy on their floor. This "Golden Sample" is the final authority. If the bulk production arrives and doesn't match the sealed sample, you have the right to a refund or rework.

Finalizing the "Bulk Production Order": Approval of the PPS usually triggers the final fabric purchase and the scheduling of the production line. Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What are the different types of clothing samples?
The main stages include Proto Samples (design check), Fit Samples (sizing check), Size Sets (grading check), and PPS (Pre-Production Samples) which are the final reference for bulk production.

Q2: How long does the sampling process take in China?
Each sampling round typically takes 7-12 days for creation plus 3-5 days for international shipping. A full development cycle often involves 2-3 rounds.

Q3: What is a 'Golden Sample'?
A Golden Sample is the final, approved PPS that has been signed off by the brand. It serves as the physical standard against which all bulk production units are compared.

Q4: Should I pay for samples?
Yes, manufacturers usually charge 2x to 3x the bulk unit price for samples to cover the high labor cost of making a single piece. This fee is often refundable once a bulk order is placed.

Q5: How do I give feedback on a sample?
Use clear photos with annotations, provide side-by-side comparisons with tech packs, and use a standardized 'Comment Sheet' to list all required revisions.
