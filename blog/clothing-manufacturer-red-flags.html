10 Red Flags When Sourcing Clothing from China (And How to Avoid Scams)

Sourcing clothing from China offers incredible opportunities for margin and scale, but it is not without risks. For every honest, high-quality factory, there is a middleman or a "ghost factory" waiting to take advantage of inexperienced buyers. Losing a deposit or receiving a container full of unsellable rags can destroy a small brand. In this article, we draw from our years of experience on the factory floor to identify the top 10 clothing manufacturer red flags. Knowing these signs will help you filter out the "scammers" and find a partner who truly supports your brand's growth.

1. Prices That Are Too Good to Be True

The most common trap for new brands is the "low-cost lure." If you get quotes from five factories and one is 40% cheaper than the others, it is almost certainly a red flag. In the garment industry, raw material costs (fabric, thread, zippers) and labor costs are relatively standard. A significantly lower price usually means the supplier is using inferior fabric, skipping quality control, or—worse—planning to disappear once the deposit is paid.

2. Vague or Non-Existent Physical Address

A legitimate factory will have a clear physical address in a known manufacturing hub like Guangdong (Guangzhou, Dongguan, Shenzhen), Zhejiang, or Jiangsu. If their website only lists a "Room 802, Building B" without a street name or district, be wary. Use Google Maps or Baidu Maps to verify the location. If the address points to a residential apartment building or a shopping mall, they are a trading company, not a factory.

3. Refusing Third-Party Inspections or Audits

This is a "hard" red flag. Any professional factory in China is accustomed to visits from inspection agencies like SGS, Intertek, or QIMA. If a supplier makes excuses—"Our factory is in a sensitive area" or "We are too busy for visitors"—it's time to walk away. Honest manufacturers take pride in their facility and welcome audits as a way to prove their reliability.

4. Pressure to Pay via Insecure Methods

Scammers love Western Union, MoneyGram, and personal bank accounts. These methods provide zero recourse for the buyer. A professional business will always have a corporate bank account (often a "Hong Kong Limited" or a Mainland Chinese account) that matches their business license. If they ask you to send "friends and family" payments via PayPal to avoid fees, they are not a professional manufacturer.

5. "Jack of All Trades" Product Catalog

Real factories specialize. A factory that makes high-performance gym leggings usually doesn't make winter coats, leather bags, and children's toys. If their catalog looks like a supermarket, they are a trading company. While trading companies can be useful for small MOQs, they often lack control over the production line and will struggle to maintain consistent quality across different product categories.

6. Lack of Technical Knowledge

When you ask detailed questions about fabric GSM (grams per square meter), dye fastness, or seam types (like flatlock vs. overlock), a factory representative should be able to answer immediately. If they are vague or keep saying "Yes, we can do everything" without explaining *how*, they likely don't understand the technical side of garment construction.

7. Inconsistent Communication and Response Times

While everyone has off days, a supplier who takes 4 days to answer a simple email during the negotiation phase will be a nightmare during production. If their communication is sporadic before they have your money, imagine how hard they will be to reach once they have your deposit. Look for "Gold Supplier" status on Alibaba or active social media presences that show ongoing work.

8. Fake or Expired Certifications

Many suppliers post BSCI, OEKO-TEX, or ISO certificates on their profiles. Scammers often Photoshop these. Check the certificate number on the official issuing body's website. If the name on the certificate doesn't match the company name, or if it expired in 2019, the supplier is being dishonest about their compliance standards.

9. Unwillingness to Provide Samples

Samples are a standard part of the process. If a factory insists that you "trust them" and go straight to bulk because "samples take too much time," do not proceed. Every professional relationship starts with a sample to verify fit, fabric, and finish. A factory that won't make a sample is a factory that can't make the product.

10. The Name Change Game

If you search for a company name and find bad reviews or legal disputes, and then the representative tells you "We have a new name now," be extremely careful. While companies do rebrand, "phoenix companies" often rise from the ashes of a scam to catch new victims. Always verify the age of the business license.
