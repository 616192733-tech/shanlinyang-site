How to Get a Quote from a Chinese Clothing Factory: Template & Tips

Starting a fashion brand is an exciting journey, but the first hurdle is often the most daunting: how to get a quote from a clothing factory in China that is both accurate and competitive. In this guide, we break down the professional process of requesting quotes to ensure you get the best possible terms for your production.

When you're looking to scale your apparel business, China remains the global powerhouse for garment manufacturing. However, many brands fail to get a quote from a clothing factory in China because their initial inquiry is too vague. Factories are busy, and they prioritize inquiries that show the brand is professional and ready to produce.

To help you navigate this, we've compiled this comprehensive guide. We'll cover everything from the documents you need to have ready, to the specific questions you should ask, and even provide an email template that you can copy and paste today.

1. Preparation: What You Need Before You Ask for a Quote

Before you even send an email, you need to have your "ducks in a row." A factory cannot give you a price if they don't know what they are making. The more information you provide, the less risk the factory perceives, and the lower your initial price is likely to be.

You should have a clear idea of your target market, your price point, and your expected sales volume. If you approach a factory saying, "I want to make a t-shirt," you'll get a very different response than if you say, "I want to produce 500 units of a 240gsm heavy cotton oversized t-shirt with a screen-printed logo."

Essential prep items include:

  • Market Research: Know what your competitors are selling and at what price.
  • Budgeting: Understand your maximum landed cost per unit.
  • Timeline: When do you need the goods in your warehouse?

2. The Tech Pack: Why It’s Your Most Important Document

If you want to know how to get a quote from a clothing factory in China that actually sticks, you need a Tech Pack. A Tech Pack is the "blueprint" of your garment. It contains all the technical specifications required to manufacture the item.

Without a Tech Pack, the factory has to guess. And when factories guess, they guess high to protect themselves from unexpected costs. A professional Tech Pack should include:

  • Technical Drawings: Front and back views with callouts.
  • Bill of Materials (BOM): A list of every fabric, thread, button, and label.
  • Size Specification Sheet: Measurements for every size in your range.
  • Artwork details: Placement, size, and method for logos or prints.

If you don't have a professional Tech Pack, consider hiring a freelance designer or a production agency to create one for you. It will save you thousands of dollars in the long run.

3. Understanding the Quote Breakdown: Fabrics, Labor, and Trims

When you receive a quote, it's rarely just one number. A professional factory will provide a breakdown. Understanding this breakdown is key to negotiating effectively.

The total cost is typically composed of:

  • Fabric Cost: This is usually 50-70% of the total cost. The quality of the fabric (GSM, composition, finish) is the biggest driver.
  • Labor Cost (CMT): CMT stands for Cut, Make, and Trim. This is the cost of the actual sewing and assembly.
  • Trims & Accessories: Zippers, buttons, elastic, and labels.
  • Packaging: Polybags, hangtags, and cartons.
  • Factory Margin: The profit the factory makes on the order.

By seeing these components, you can identify where costs are coming from. If the quote is too high, you might decide to change the fabric or simplify the construction rather than just asking for a blind discount.

4. Common Pitfalls When Requesting Quotes from Chinese Factories

Many new brand owners make the same mistakes when trying to get a quote from a clothing factory in China. Here are the most common ones to avoid:

Asking for a quote without quantities: Pricing is heavily dependent on volume. Always provide an estimated quantity (e.g., "I'm looking for 200 pieces per style").

Using vague language: Phrases like "high quality" or "soft fabric" are subjective. Use technical terms like "long-staple cotton" or "double-stitched seams."

Ignoring the MOQ: Every factory has a Minimum Order Quantity. If your order is too small, the factory may either decline the quote or charge a significant "small order surcharge."

Not accounting for shipping: The price you get is usually FOB (Free On Board) or EXW (Ex Works). Make sure you calculate the cost of getting the goods from the factory to your door.

5. Sample Inquiry Email Template for Professional Results

To make your life easier, here is a template you can use to contact factories. It is designed to show that you are a serious, professional buyer.

Subject: Inquiry: Custom Apparel Production for [Your Brand Name] - [Product Type]

Dear Shanlinyang Apparel Team,

My name is [Your Name], and I am the founder of [Your Brand Name], based in [Your Country]. We specialize in [Your Niche, e.g., high-end fitness wear].

We have reviewed your factory profile and are interested in partnering with you for our upcoming collection. Attached, you will find the Tech Packs for our [Product Name].

We are looking for a quote based on the following details:

  • Estimated Quantity: [e.g., 300 units per style / 100 units per color]
  • Fabric Requirement: [e.g., 75% Nylon / 25% Spandex, 230gsm]
  • Timeline: We would like to start production by [Month] for a [Month] delivery.
  • Shipping Terms: Please provide pricing based on [FOB or DAP].

Could you also please provide your current lead times for sampling and bulk production?

I look forward to hearing from you.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Your Brand Website]
