How to Trademark Your Clothing Brand: Labels, Logos & IP Protection

PUBLISHED 2026-06-29 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~13 MIN READ

Building a clothing brand is about more than just great designs and high-quality manufacturing. It's about building an identity that customers trust and recognize. However, many entrepreneurs make the mistake of launching without securing their intellectual property (IP). This clothing brand trademark guide is designed to help you navigate the essential steps of protecting your brand name, logo, and designs before you become a target for copycats or legal disputes.

In the global fashion market, a trademark is one of your most valuable assets. It distinguishes your goods from competitors and allows you to build a loyal following. Without a registered trademark, you could spend years and thousands of dollars building a brand, only to be forced to change your name because someone else registered it first. At Shanlinyang Apparel, we understand that our clients' success depends on a secure brand identity. Let's explore how to protect your label from day one.

1. Why Trademarking is Critical for New Clothing Brands in the Global Market

The fashion industry is notoriously competitive and fast-moving. New trends are copied within weeks, and popular brand names are often "squatted" by bad actors. Here is why every clothing brand trademark guide emphasizes early registration:

Preventing Consumer Confusion: A trademark ensures that when a customer sees your logo on a tag, they know it comes from your brand and meets your quality standards. If another brand uses a similar name or logo, it dilutes your brand's value and can lead to lost sales. Trademarks give you the legal power to stop others from confusing your customers.

Building a Saleable Asset: If you ever plan to sell your clothing brand or seek investment, your trademark portfolio is one of the first things a buyer will look at. A registered trademark proves that you own your identity and that your business has a clear "moat" against competitors. It transforms your "brand" into a legally recognized piece of property.

Amazon Brand Registry and Social Media Protection: Platforms like Amazon, Instagram, and TikTok have strict IP protection policies. If you want to use the Amazon Brand Registry—which gives you control over your product listings and access to advanced marketing tools—you must have a registered trademark. Similarly, having a trademark makes it much easier to take down "copycat" social media accounts that are using your brand name.

International Expansion: If you plan to sell globally, you need to think about international trademark protection. Many countries operate on a "first-to-file" basis, meaning whoever registers the name first owns it, regardless of who used it first. Protecting your brand early ensures you can expand into new markets without legal roadblocks.

2. Step-by-Step: Searching and Registering Your Clothing Brand Trademark

The process of trademarking can be technical, but it generally follows four main steps. This is the core of any clothing brand trademark guide:

Step 1: Conduct a Comprehensive Search: Before you file, you must ensure your brand name isn't already in use. Don't just search Google. Use official databases like the USPTO's TESS (in the US) or the WIPO Global Brand Database. Search for exact matches and "confusingly similar" names. If your brand is "Blue Moon Clothing," search for "BlueMoon," "Blue-Moon," and even "Luna Azul."

Step 2: Choose Your Trademark Class: Trademarks are filed in specific "classes" based on the goods or services you provide. For clothing brands, the primary class is **Class 25** (Clothing, footwear, headwear). If you also sell accessories like jewelry, you'll need **Class 14**. Bags and leather goods fall under **Class 18**. Each class requires a separate filing fee, so choose wisely based on your current and near-future product lines.

Step 3: File Your Application: In the US, you'll use the Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS). You will need to provide a "specimen" of your trademark in use—this could be a photo of your brand name on a neck label or a hangtag. If you haven't started selling yet, you can file an "Intent to Use" (ITU) application to reserve the name.

Step 4: Examination and Publication: A trademark examiner will review your application. If it meets all legal requirements, it will be "published for opposition" for 30 days. This gives others a chance to object if they believe your trademark infringes on theirs. If no one opposes, your trademark will proceed to registration.

3. Trademarking Your Logo vs. Your Brand Name: Which Comes First?

A common question we see at Shanlinyang Apparel is: "Should I trademark my logo or my brand name first?" While both are important, they offer different types of protection:

The Brand Name (Standard Character Mark): This is usually the highest priority in any clothing brand trademark guide. Registering your brand name as a "standard character mark" protects the name itself, regardless of the font, color, or style. This means if you decide to redesign your logo in two years, your brand name remains protected. It provides the broadest possible shield for your identity.

The Logo (Design Mark): A logo trademark protects the specific visual representation of your brand. If your brand is heavily focused on a unique icon (like the Nike Swoosh or the Adidas Trefoil), protecting the design mark is essential. However, keep in mind that if you change the logo significantly, you may need to file a new trademark application.

The Combined Strategy: Ideally, as your brand grows, you should protect both. Start with the name, then protect your primary logo. If you have a specific "sub-brand" or a recurring graphic that you use on all your apparel, consider protecting those as well. This "layered" protection makes it much harder for competitors to infringe on any part of your brand identity.

4. How to Protect Your Intellectual Property When Manufacturing in China

Many clothing brands manufacture in China, and it is a common misconception that IP cannot be protected there. In fact, China has a robust trademark system, but it operates differently than in the West. This clothing brand trademark guide wouldn't be complete without addressing manufacturing protection:

Register Your Trademark in China: Even if you don't plan to sell your products in the Chinese domestic market, you should register your trademark in China. Why? Because China is a "first-to-file" country. If a squatter registers your brand name in China, they can legally seize your goods at the border as they are being exported from the factory to your home country. This is a common tactic used to extort money from successful brands. Protecting your mark in China is a low-cost insurance policy for your supply chain.

Use Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) and NNN Agreements: When working with a manufacturer, don't just use a standard Western NDA. Use a Chinese-language **NNN Agreement** (Non-use, Non-disclosure, Non-circumvention). This prevents the factory from using your designs to create products for other customers or selling your goods directly on platforms like Taobao or AliExpress.

Control Your Labels and Trims: One way to prevent unauthorized production is to control the source of your branded materials. You can have your main brand labels manufactured at a separate facility than your garments and have them delivered to the garment factory in exact quantities. This makes it much harder for a factory to produce "overrun" stock to sell on the side.

5. Enforcing Your Trademark: Dealing with Counterfeits and Infringements

A trademark is only as good as your willingness to enforce it. If you find someone using your brand name or logo without permission, you must take action to maintain your legal rights. Here is how to handle infringements:

Cease and Desist Letters: Often, an infringement is unintentional. A formal "Cease and Desist" letter from an attorney is usually enough to stop small-scale copycats. It notifies the infringer of your rights and gives them a deadline to stop using the mark and remove any infringing products from sale.

Platform Take-Down Notices: If you find infringements on Amazon, eBay, Shopify, or Instagram, use their internal IP reporting tools. These platforms take trademark infringement very seriously and will often remove the infringing listings within days of a valid report. Having your registration certificate ready is key to this process.

Customs Recordation: In many countries, you can record your registered trademark with the customs authorities. This allows customs officers to identify and seize counterfeit goods as they enter or leave the country. This is a powerful tool for preventing large-scale distribution of fake products.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: Do I need to trademark my clothing brand name?
Yes, trademarking provides legal protection against others using your brand name or logo. It is essential for building a long-term asset and protecting your market share.

Q2: What is the USPTO class for clothing?
The primary class for clothing, footwear, and headwear is Class 25. If you sell bags or jewelry, you may also need Class 18 or Class 14.

Q3: Should I trademark my logo or brand name first?
Generally, it is recommended to trademark the brand name (standard character mark) first, as it provides the broadest protection regardless of font or color.

Q4: How much does it cost to trademark a clothing brand?
In the US, government filing fees usually start at around $250-$350 per class. Legal fees vary if you hire an attorney to handle the filing.

Q5: Can I trademark a clothing brand name if I haven't started selling yet?
Yes, you can file an 'Intent to Use' (ITU) application, which allows you to reserve the name while you prepare for launch.
