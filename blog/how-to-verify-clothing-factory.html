Factory verification guide

How to verify a clothing factory in China before you pay a deposit.

Use this 15-point due diligence checklist to reduce supplier risk before ordering womenswear, activewear, dresses, swimwear, plus-size apparel, or private-label clothing from a Chinese manufacturer.

Short answer

To verify a Chinese clothing factory, do not rely on price and catalog photos alone. Check the business license, factory address, product specialization, live video tour, sample quality, QC process, payment terms, export experience, bank account match, and willingness to provide evidence before bulk production.

Why factory verification matters

A fashion brand can recover from a slow sample or a revised measurement spec. It is much harder to recover from choosing the wrong production partner, paying a deposit to an unqualified supplier, or discovering after shipment that the product does not meet your fit, fabric, label, or packing standard.

Verification is not only about avoiding scams. It is about confirming whether the supplier can actually handle your category, quantity, target market, quality expectations, and delivery route. A good activewear supplier may not be the right dress supplier. A stock wholesaler may not be ready for private-label packaging. A trading company may be useful for mixed sourcing, but you should know exactly who controls production.

The 15-point clothing factory verification checklist

Confirm the registered company name. Ask for the business license and check whether the company name matches emails, invoices, contracts, and bank account information.
Check business scope. The license should reasonably match apparel production, garments, textiles, export, trading, or related business activities.
Verify the physical address. Compare the address on the license, website, Alibaba storefront, video call, and shipping documents.
Ask whether they are a factory or trading company. Neither is automatically bad, but the answer should be clear and consistent.
Test category specialization. Ask technical questions about your exact product: leggings opacity, dress lining, swimwear elastic, plus-size grading, label placement, or washing shrinkage.
Request a live video tour. Look for cutting tables, sewing lines, sample room, fabric storage, QC area, packing area, and finished goods.
Review real samples. Do not judge only by catalog photos. Inspect stitching, measurements, fabric handfeel, stretch recovery, labels, and packing.
Ask for the sample process. A serious factory can explain sample fee, timeline, revision process, fit comments, and pre-production sample approval.
Check MOQ logic. MOQ should depend on fabric, color, trim, logo method, pattern complexity, and cutting efficiency—not a random number.
Ask for QC steps. Confirm measurement checks, fabric confirmation, inline checks, final inspection, packing photos, and tolerance standards.
Clarify payment terms. Common terms are deposit before production and balance after inspection but before shipment. Be careful with 100% upfront pressure.
Match bank details. The receiving account should match the agreed company or a clearly explained platform/payment route.
Confirm export and delivery experience. Ask about US, Canada, UK, EU, Amazon FBA, DDP, 3PL, carton marks, barcode labels, and polybag requirements if relevant.
Document everything. Keep quote sheets, sample comments, fabric approvals, size specs, label files, packing rules, and delivery terms in writing.
Start with a controlled first order. For a new supplier, reduce risk with a focused style set, sample approval, QC checkpoints, and clear acceptance criteria.

Red flags when sourcing clothing from China

Commercial red flags

  • Price is far below every other quote.
  • Supplier pushes urgent payment before answering technical questions.
  • They refuse normal sample or inspection steps.
  • They promise every category, every fabric, and every MOQ without limits.
  • They avoid written confirmation of specs, packing, or payment terms.

Production red flags

  • No live factory tour or vague workshop photos.
  • Sales team cannot explain fabric weight, shrinkage, construction, or labels.
  • Sample quality is inconsistent with promised bulk quality.
  • No measurement tolerance or QC process.
  • Bank account, company name, or address does not match without a clear reason.

How to test whether a factory understands your project

Send a short but specific sourcing brief. Include product reference photos or a tech pack, target fabric, size range, quantity by color, logo and label needs, packaging, destination country, warehouse or marketplace requirements, and launch deadline.

A capable factory should respond with practical questions, not only a price. For example: fabric availability, pattern complexity, sample route, MOQ by color, label method, packaging details, production lead time, and QC checkpoints.

Factory verification FAQ

How do I verify a clothing factory in China?

Check the business license, registered name, address, category specialization, sample quality, live video tour, QC process, payment terms, export experience, and whether the bank account matches the company information.

Should I always visit the factory before ordering?

A visit is helpful but not always necessary for early-stage buyers. A live video tour, third-party inspection, sample review, written specs, and staged payment terms can reduce risk before a first order.

Is the lowest quote usually dangerous?

Not always, but a quote far below the market should be investigated. It may hide cheaper fabric, missing packaging, weak QC, wrong MOQ assumptions, or extra costs later.

What should I check before paying the balance?

Check bulk photos, measurements, labels, packing, carton marks, quantity, color, size ratio, and inspection results before paying the final balance and arranging shipment.
