China Clothing Tariffs & Import Duties: 2025 Guide for US & EU Brands

PUBLISHED 2025-06-27 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~9 MIN READ

For fashion brands sourcing from China, tariffs and import duties are often the largest variable in the financial equation. With global trade policies constantly evolving, staying informed about the latest regulations in 2025 is essential for maintaining your profit margins. This guide provides a deep dive into the tariff landscape for the United States and the European Union, offering practical advice on how to navigate these costs effectively.

1. How Tariffs Affect Your Clothing Production Costs

When you receive a quote from a factory in China, it is typically an FOB (Free on Board) or EXW (Ex-Works) price. This does not include the cost of getting the goods into your country. Tariffs and duties are taxes imposed by your local government on imported goods. For clothing, these rates are among the highest for any product category, often ranging from 10% to over 30% of the value of the goods.

It is vital to calculate these costs before placing an order. A garment that costs $10 to manufacture might end up costing you $15 after shipping, duty, and taxes. This is your "landed cost," and it should be the basis for your retail pricing strategy. Failing to account for tariffs can lead to "phantom" profits that disappear once the customs bill arrives.

2. HS Codes Explained: Classifying Your Garments Correctly

The Harmonized System (HS) is an international nomenclature for the classification of products. Every item imported is assigned a specific HS code. For apparel, the classification is extremely granular. For example, a "knitted cotton T-shirt" has a different code than a "woven silk blouse."

The duty rate you pay depends entirely on the HS code. Factors that influence the code include:

  • Fabric Construction: Knitted vs. Woven.
  • Material Composition: Cotton, Polyester, Silk, Wool, etc.
  • Garment Type: Shirts, Trousers, Dresses, Jackets, etc.
  • Target User: Men's, Women's, or Children's.
Work closely with your factory or a customs broker to ensure your garments are classified correctly. Misclassification can lead to paying too much duty or, worse, being fined by customs for underpayment.

3. Current Tariff Rates for the US (Section 301 and beyond)

For US-based brands, the tariff landscape is dominated by the "Section 301" tariffs on Chinese-origin goods. These tariffs were introduced in several "lists" and have added significantly to the cost of importing from China. As of 2025, many apparel items are subject to an additional 7.5% to 25% tariff on top of the base MFN (Most Favored Nation) duty rates found in the Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS).

The base duty rates for apparel in the US are already high—often between 10% and 32%. When you add the Section 301 tariffs, the total duty can exceed 40% for certain items. However, many e-commerce brands utilize the "Section 321" (de minimis) rule, which allows for duty-free entry of shipments valued at $800 or less per person per day. This is a common strategy for direct-to-consumer (DTC) brands shipping individual orders directly from China.

4. Import Duties and VAT in the European Union

The EU has a different approach. While it doesn't currently have a "Section 301" equivalent for Chinese clothing, the base duty rates and VAT (Value Added Tax) can still be significant. The standard duty rate for most apparel imported into the EU is 12%.

VAT is the more significant factor. VAT is charged on the "taxable value" of the shipment, which is the value of the goods + shipping costs + duty. Standard VAT rates in the EU range from 17% (Luxembourg) to 27% (Hungary), with most major markets like Germany and France at 19-20%. Unlike duty, VAT is often "recoverable" for businesses registered for VAT in the EU, meaning it acts more like a cash-flow item than a direct cost, but it still needs to be managed carefully.

5. Strategies to Legally Minimize Tariff Impact

While you must always comply with the law, there are several legal strategies to minimize your tariff burden:

  • First Sale Valuation: For large volumes, you may be able to base the duty on the price paid by a middleman to the factory, rather than the price you pay to the middleman. This requires complex legal structuring but can save millions.
  • Duty Drawback: If you import goods into the US or EU and then re-export them to another country, you can often claim a refund of the duties paid.
  • Bonded Warehouses: Storing goods in a bonded warehouse allows you to delay payment of duties and VAT until the goods are actually moved into the local market.
  • Tariff Engineering: Small changes in design or fabric composition can sometimes move a garment from a high-duty HS code to a lower-duty one. For example, adding a certain percentage of a different fiber might change the classification.

Navigating the world of tariffs requires a proactive approach. By understanding the rules and planning ahead, you can protect your brand's profitability while continuing to benefit from the high-quality manufacturing available in China.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are Section 301 tariffs on Chinese clothing?

Section 301 tariffs are additional duties imposed by the US government on certain Chinese imports, including many categories of apparel. These are on top of the base duty rates.

How do I find the HS code for my garment?

You can find HS codes in the Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) of your country. Your factory or a customs broker can also help identify the correct code based on fabric and garment type.

Do I have to pay VAT on clothing imported into the EU?

Yes, all goods imported into the EU are subject to Value Added Tax (VAT), usually at the rate of the destination country. This is calculated on the value of the goods plus shipping and duties.

Can I lower my import duties legally?

Yes, strategies include ensuring correct HS code classification, using Free Trade Agreements if applicable, and utilizing programs like First Sale Valuation (for large volumes).

What is the de minimis threshold in the US?

The US de minimis threshold (Section 321) allows for duty-free entry of shipments valued at $800 or less per person per day. This is widely used by e-commerce brands for direct-to-consumer shipping.
