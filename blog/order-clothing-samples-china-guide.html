How to Order Clothing Samples from China: Step-by-Step for First-Time Buyers

For many first-time fashion entrepreneurs, the prospect of ordering samples from a factory thousands of miles away can be daunting. Yet, the sampling phase is the most critical part of your production journey. It is the bridge between your imagination and a physical, sellable product. In this guide, we demark the sampling process into five clear, manageable steps to ensure your first experience with a Chinese manufacturer is a success.

1. Why Sampling is the Most Critical Step in Your Production Journey

Think of a clothing sample as a prototype. Just as an engineer wouldn't build a bridge without a model, you shouldn't launch a clothing line without samples. The sampling process serves three vital functions:

Verification of Quality: You get to see, feel, and wear the garment. You can check the fabric weight, the elasticity, and whether the color looks the same in person as it did on your screen. Fit Testing: Every brand has a different "ideal" fit. Sampling allows you to ensure your medium actually fits like a medium, and that the garment is comfortable during movement—especially important for activewear. Communication Check: The sample is the ultimate proof of how well you and your factory understand each other. If the sample comes back perfectly, you've found a great partner. If there are issues, it's an opportunity to refine your communication before thousands of dollars are on the line.

Skipping or rushing this step is the number one cause of production disasters. At Shanlinyang Apparel, we always insist on a finalized "Pre-Production Sample" (PPS) being signed off by the client before we ever start the bulk machines.

2. Phase 1: Preparing Your Tech Pack and Design Specifications

Before you even contact a factory, you need to "speak the language" of manufacturing. This means having a Tech Pack. A tech pack is a comprehensive document that includes:

  • Technical Drawings (Flats): 2D drawings showing the front, back, and side views of the garment.
  • Bill of Materials (BOM): A list of every single thing that goes into the garment—fabric, thread, buttons, zippers, labels, and even the type of polybag for packaging.
  • Size Chart (Grading): Measurements for every size you intend to produce.
  • Logo/Graphic Placement: Exact coordinates for where your branding should go.

If you don't have a professional tech pack, don't panic. Many factories (including ours) can work from a physical "reference sample." You buy a garment you like from another brand, send it to us, and tell us: "I want this fabric, but with this measurement change and this logo placement." This is often the fastest and most accurate way for startups to begin.

3. Phase 2: Communicating Your Vision to the Chinese Factory

Clear communication is the secret sauce of successful sourcing. When you reach out to a manufacturer like Shanlinyang Apparel, be as specific as possible. Instead of saying "I want a soft blue fabric," say "I want a 220gsm Nylon/Spandex blend in Pantone 19-4052 Classic Blue."

Use Visuals: A picture is worth a thousand words, especially when dealing with language barriers. Use arrows and annotations on photos to point out exactly what you want. Confirm Sizing: Don't assume "Medium" is universal. China sizes, US sizes, and EU sizes are all different. Always provide an actual measurement table in centimeters or inches. Discuss Sample Costs: Be prepared to pay a sample fee. This covers the pattern maker's time and the disruption of the production line to make just one item. At Shanlinyang, we typically refund the sample fee once your bulk order reaches a certain threshold.

4. Phase 3: Reviewing the Sample and Providing Actionable Feedback

When that DHL package finally arrives, it's tempting to just tear it open and put it on. Instead, take a systematic approach to your review:

  1. Measure Everything: Lay the garment flat and check every dimension against your size chart. Note any discrepancies.
  2. Check Construction: Turn the garment inside out. Are the seams clean? Is there any "bird's nesting" (tangled thread)? Pull on the seams to see if they hold up.
  3. The Wash Test: Wash and dry the garment according to the care label. Does it shrink? Does the color bleed? Does the fabric pill?
  4. The Fit Test: Have a fit model (or yourself, if you are the target audience) wear the garment. Does it pinch? Does it ride up? For activewear, perform a "squat test" in front of a mirror to check for transparency.

When providing feedback, be constructive. Instead of "I don't like the fit," say "The waistband needs to be 2cm higher and the leg opening 1cm narrower." This gives the factory something they can actually fix.

5. Common Sampling Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Even with a good guide, mistakes happen. Here are the top three pitfalls first-time buyers face:

Pitfall 1: Expecting "Proto-Samples" to be Perfect. The first sample is often made with "available" fabric and colors just to test the fit and construction. Don't reject a factory because the first sample used a slightly different shade of red than you wanted—as long as the construction is solid.

Pitfall 2: Too Many Changes at Once. If you change the fabric, the fit, and the design all in one go, you won't know which change caused an issue if the next sample comes back wrong. Iterate systematically.

Pitfall 3: Not Factoring in Shipping Time. Each round of sampling takes 2-3 weeks. If you need three rounds of samples, that's over two months. Factor this into your launch timeline so you aren't rushing and making poor decisions as your deadline approaches.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a clothing sample usually cost?

Sampling costs typically range from $50 to $150 per style. This is usually 2-3 times the bulk production price because it covers the cost of pattern making, fabric sourcing for a single unit, and specialized labor. Many factories refund this cost if you proceed to a bulk order.

How long does it take to get a sample from China?

On average, it takes 7 to 15 days for the factory to make the sample, plus 3 to 7 days for international shipping via DHL or FedEx. Total turnaround is usually 2-3 weeks.

Do I need a tech pack to order a sample?

While a tech pack is highly recommended as it acts as a blueprint, you can also send a physical 'reference sample' (an existing garment you want to replicate or modify) along with clear notes on changes.

Can I request changes after the first sample?

Absolutely. This is called the 'feedback loop.' Most brands go through 1-2 rounds of sampling before the 'Pre-Production Sample' (PPS) is approved for bulk manufacturing.

What should I look for when I receive my sample?

Check for fabric quality, color accuracy, stitching strength (pull the seams!), measurement accuracy against your size chart, and the overall 'hand feel' of the garment.
