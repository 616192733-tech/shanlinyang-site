Clothing Manufacturing Contract Guide: What to Include Before You Pay a Deposit

PUBLISHED 2025-06-27 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~9 MIN READ

In the apparel industry, a well-drafted manufacturing contract is your most important shield. Many brands rely on informal emails or verbal agreements, only to face significant challenges when things go wrong. Whether it's quality defects, late deliveries, or design theft, a formal contract provides the framework for resolution. This guide details exactly what should be in your clothing manufacturing contract before you wire that first deposit to a Chinese factory.

1. The Importance of a Bilingual Manufacturing Contract

If you are working with a factory in China, the most critical aspect of your contract is that it is bilingual. While English is the international language of business, a contract written only in English may not be enforceable in a Chinese court. A bilingual contract, with both English and Chinese text, ensures that both the factory owner and the legal system can understand the terms clearly. Usually, the contract will state that the Chinese version prevails in case of a discrepancy, which is a standard practice for international trade within China.

Having a bilingual contract also demonstrates that you are a serious, professional brand. It shows the factory that you understand the local legal environment and are prepared to protect your interests. This alone can act as a deterrent for suppliers who might otherwise be tempted to prioritize larger clients or cut corners on your production run.

2. Essential Clauses: Scope of Work and Quality Standards

The "Scope of Work" section should be as detailed as possible. Don't just say "1,000 t-shirts." Specify the fabric weight (GSM), fabric composition (e.g., 95% cotton, 5% spandex), stitching type, and any embellishments like embroidery or screen printing. Refer to your tech pack as an official appendix to the contract.

Equally important are the "Quality Standards." Define what constitutes a "major" vs. a "minor" defect. Reference international standards like AQL 2.5/4.0 for inspections. State clearly that the goods must match the approved pre-production sample (PPS) in every detail. If the bulk production doesn't meet these standards, the contract should outline the factory's obligation to repair, replace, or refund the affected items.

3. Payment Terms and Deposit Protection

Payment terms are often the most debated part of a contract. The industry standard for custom manufacturing is a 30% deposit and 70% balance. The 30% deposit allows the factory to purchase raw materials and book production time. The 70% balance should only be paid after you have received a satisfactory inspection report from a third-party QC agency or after you have personally approved high-resolution photos and videos of the finished, packed goods.

To protect your deposit, ensure the contract specifies the bank account details of the factory. The name on the bank account must match the name on the factory's business license. Avoid paying into personal accounts. If you are using Alibaba Trade Assurance, mention this in the contract as it provides an additional layer of escrow-like protection for your funds.

4. Lead Times and Late Delivery Penalties

Time is money in fashion. Missing a seasonal launch can be catastrophic. Your contract must specify a clear "Cargo Ready Date." This is the date by which the goods must be finished, inspected, and ready for pickup. Don't just accept a vague range like "30-45 days."

Include a "Liquidated Damages" or penalty clause for late delivery. A common term is a 1% to 2% reduction in the total order value for every week the shipment is delayed beyond the agreed date. This gives the factory a financial incentive to stay on schedule and prioritize your order. Of course, exceptions should be made for "Force Majeure" events (like natural disasters), but these must be clearly defined.

5. Intellectual Property and Design Ownership

Design theft is a valid concern for many brands. To protect your IP, include a Non-Disclosure, Non-Use, and Non-Circumvention (NNN) clause. This prohibits the factory from:

  • Sharing your designs or tech packs with third parties.
  • Using your patterns or molds to produce items for themselves or other clients.
  • Selling your overstock or "seconds" to other buyers without your permission.

Clarify that you own all intellectual property rights to the designs, logos, and patterns created for the order. This ensures that even if you decide to switch factories in the future, you have the legal right to take your designs with you.

By including these five pillars in your manufacturing contract, you move from a position of vulnerability to one of strength. A good factory, like Shanlinyang Apparel, will welcome a clear contract because it protects them as well, ensuring that expectations are managed and payments are made on time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I need a formal contract with a Chinese factory?

A formal contract clearly defines the expectations for quality, delivery times, and payment terms. It provides a legal basis for dispute resolution and ensures both parties are on the same page.

What are the typical payment terms for clothing manufacturing?

The most common payment terms are a 30% deposit upfront and the remaining 70% balance paid after the final inspection but before shipment.

Should the contract be in English or Chinese?

For contracts to be enforceable in Chinese courts, they should be bilingual (English and Chinese), with the Chinese version typically prevailing in case of conflict.

How can I protect my designs from being copied?

Include a non-disclosure and non-compete (NNN) clause in your contract, which prohibits the factory from sharing your designs or selling them to other clients.

What happens if the delivery is late?

The contract should include a penalty clause for late delivery, such as a percentage reduction in the total price for every week of delay.
