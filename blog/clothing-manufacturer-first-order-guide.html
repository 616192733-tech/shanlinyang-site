First production order guide

Your first clothing order with a China manufacturer: a practical buyer checklist.

Use this roadmap before paying a deposit or moving from sample to bulk. It is written for fashion startups, boutique buyers, Shopify brands, Amazon sellers, and private-label teams sourcing womenswear, activewear, dresses, swimwear, and plus-size apparel.

Short answer

The safest first order is the smallest commercially useful order with a clear sourcing brief, approved sample, confirmed fabric and size specs, written packing rules, staged payment terms, and inspection before the final balance.

What usually goes wrong in a first clothing order?

Most first-order problems come from unclear assumptions: the buyer thinks the factory understood the fit, the factory thinks the buyer approved the fabric, the label file is sent too late, or the final inspection happens after the balance is paid.

A strong first order turns those assumptions into written checkpoints. The goal is to make the buyer, factory, and logistics route follow the same approved standard before goods leave the factory.

First order roadmap: idea to shipment

Send a specific sourcing brief

Include product photos or a tech pack, target fabric, quantity by color and size, destination country, label and packaging needs, delivery deadline, and whether goods go to your warehouse, Amazon FBA, or a 3PL.

Verify the factory fit

Check whether the supplier understands your category. Activewear, dresses, swimwear, plus-size apparel, and boutique stock orders need different production knowledge.

Confirm MOQ and price logic

MOQ depends on fabric, color, cutting efficiency, trim, logo method, label requirements, and construction complexity. Ask what changes would raise or lower the MOQ.

Approve the sample route

Do not skip samples. Confirm whether the first sample tests fit only, fabric only, or the full bulk standard. For private-label goods, check logo, label, hangtag, and packaging before bulk.

Lock the production standard

Before bulk cutting, confirm measurements, tolerance, fabric, color, trims, stitch details, logo placement, label placement, packing method, carton marks, and delivery route.

Inspect before final balance

Check size ratio, measurements, sewing quality, color, labels, logo placement, stains, threads, polybags, carton marks, packing list, and quantity before paying the balance.

First-order risk controls

Do this

  • Start with a focused capsule instead of too many styles.
  • Approve physical samples before bulk.
  • Keep all comments and approvals in writing.
  • Check labels and packaging before bulk packing.
  • Inspect before paying the final balance.

Avoid this

  • Choosing only by the lowest price.
  • Changing design details after fabric is cut.
  • Sending label files after production has started.
  • Paying 100% upfront without safeguards.
  • Planning FBA or 3PL requirements after packing.

Special notes for US, Canada, UK, and Europe buyers

For North American and European orders, confirm size conversion, care labels, barcode labels, polybags, carton marks, DDP/FBA/3PL requirements, warehouse delivery timing, and launch calendar buffers before production starts.

First clothing order FAQ

What is the safest way to place a first clothing order with a Chinese factory?

Use a clear sourcing brief, verify the supplier, approve a sample, confirm all specs in writing, use staged payment terms, inspect before final balance, and confirm delivery requirements before packing.

Should I order a sample before bulk production?

Yes. A sample or pre-production sample gives both buyer and factory a physical standard for fit, fabric, construction, labels, and packing.

When should labels and packaging be confirmed?

Before bulk production starts. Late labels, hangtags, barcode stickers, and carton marks often cause delays or repacking costs.

Can my first order go directly to Amazon FBA or a 3PL?

Yes, but carton marks, barcode labels, polybags, SKU/FNSKU stickers, carton size, and packing list requirements must be confirmed before packing.
