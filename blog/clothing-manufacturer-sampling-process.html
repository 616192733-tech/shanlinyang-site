How to Protect Your Payment When Ordering from a Chinese Clothing Factory

Sending thousands of dollars across the world to a company you have never visited is a daunting experience for any clothing brand owner. Payment security is the foundation of a successful sourcing strategy. Without it, you risk not just your capital, but also your product launch timeline and brand reputation. In the Chinese garment industry, payment terms are fairly standardized, but the "fine print" and verification steps are where many buyers fail. This guide explains how to structure your payments, which platforms to use, and how to spot fraudulent bank details before you hit "Send."

1. Understanding the 30/70 Standard

In the world of custom clothing manufacturing, you should almost never pay 100% upfront. The standard agreement is a 30% deposit and 70% balance. The deposit allows the factory to purchase raw materials (fabric, trims, labels). The 70% balance is your leverage. You must make it clear that the balance will only be released after a third-party quality inspection is passed. If a factory insists on 50% or more for a standard order, they may be facing cash flow issues—a major risk for you.

2. The Power of Alibaba Trade Assurance

For small to mid-sized brands, Alibaba Trade Assurance is one of the safest methods. It acts as an escrow service. You pay Alibaba, and they hold the funds until you confirm the goods have been shipped and meet the agreed-upon quality. If there is a dispute, Alibaba's team will mediate. While it carries a small transaction fee, the peace of mind is invaluable, especially for your first three orders with a new supplier. Once trust is established over a year, many brands transition to direct Bank Wire (T/T) to save on fees.

3. Verifying Bank Details: The "Pro-forma Invoice" Check

Before sending a Wire Transfer, always request a Pro-forma Invoice (PI) that includes the company's official stamp (the "Red Chop"). Verify that the bank account name is a business name, not a personal one. A common scam involves hackers intercepting a factory's email and sending "updated" bank details. If you ever receive an email saying, "Our bank is under audit, please pay this other account," CALL the factory representative on the phone to verify. Never trust bank detail changes sent only via email.

4. Using Letters of Credit (LC) for Large Orders

If your order value exceeds $30,000 to $50,000, you should consider a Letter of Credit. This is a bank-to-bank agreement. Your bank will only release the funds when the factory provides specific documents, such as the Bill of Lading, Certificate of Origin, and Inspection Report. It is the gold standard for international trade security. While it involves more paperwork and bank fees, it virtually eliminates the risk of non-shipment or blatant fraud.

5. Payment Platforms: Wise, Revolut, and Local Transfers

Many modern brands use services like Wise (formerly TransferWise) because they offer much better exchange rates than traditional banks. Some factories now have "Local Receiving Accounts" in the US or Europe provided by platforms like Payoneer or PingPong. These are generally safe as long as the account holder name matches the factory. However, always ensure that your payment reference includes your Order Number and that you save a PDF of the transfer confirmation to send to your sales rep immediately.
