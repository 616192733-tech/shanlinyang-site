Bulk Clothing Order Checklist: 15 Things to Verify Before You Pay the Balance

The "balance payment" is the most powerful tool a buyer has. Once you send that final 70%, the factory has less incentive to fix mistakes, and the goods will soon be on a boat or plane heading to you. Before you release those funds, you must ensure that everything is exactly as promised. From the quality of the stitching to the legality of the care labels, there are dozens of details that can go wrong. We have compiled a 15-point bulk clothing order checklist to help you audit your production like a pro. Use this list to communicate with your factory and your third-party inspectors.

1. The Core Quality Inspection (The Big Three)

First and foremost, you must verify the quality of the garments themselves. This covers three main areas: Visual Defects (stains, holes, frayed threads), Construction (straightness of seams, stitch density), and Fit (measurements matching the tech pack). A professional inspector will use the AQL 2.5/4.0 standard. If the number of defects exceeds the limit, the factory must stop and fix the batch. Never accept "we will fix it in the next order" as a solution.

2. Fabric Performance and Lab Testing

Even if a garment looks great, it might fail after the first wash. Verify the fabric performance. Did the factory use the agreed-upon GSM? Does the fabric pill after friction? For activewear, is it actually "squat-proof"? You can request the factory to provide an internal test report, or better yet, have your inspector send a sample to a lab like ITS or SGS for a "color fastness" and "shrinkage" test. This costs about $100-$200 but can save you from a $20,000 refund disaster.

3. Labeling and Compliance Verification

Customs authorities in the US (CBP) and EU have strict rules about clothing labels. Every garment must have: Country of Origin (e.g., Made in China), Fiber Content (e.g., 80% Nylon, 20% Spandex), and Care Instructions. Additionally, if you are selling in the US, you need an RN number or your company name on the label. Check that the labels are sewn in the correct position and that the font is legible. Incorrect labeling can lead to your entire shipment being seized at the border.

4. Packing and Carton Specifications

Shipping is expensive, and damaged goods are a loss. Verify that the inner polybags are sealed and have "suffocation warnings" if required by Amazon or your local laws. Check the master cartons: are they 5-ply (double-walled) to prevent crushing? Are the "Shipping Marks" (carton numbers, SKU, gross weight) printed clearly on at least two sides? This makes life much easier for your warehouse team when the goods arrive.

5. Document Accuracy (PI, PL, CI, BL)

Finally, check the paperwork. The Commercial Invoice (CI) and Packing List (PL) must match exactly. If the CI says 1,000 units and the PL says 998, customs will flag it. Ensure the HS Code (Harmonized System code) is correct—this determines the tariff rate you pay. Ask the factory to send you draft copies of all documents for approval *before* they issue the final ones. Once the ship leaves, the Bill of Lading (BL) is issued; ensure you receive the "Telex Release" or the original copy to claim your goods.
