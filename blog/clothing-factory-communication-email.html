Email Templates for Communicating with Clothing Factories in China

Communicating with clothing factories in China can be one of the most challenging aspects of starting a fashion brand. Language barriers, cultural differences, and the sheer volume of emails factories receive can lead to slow responses or misunderstandings. However, using professional clothing factory communication email templates can dramatically improve your success rate. A well-structured email signals that you are a serious professional, making suppliers more likely to prioritize your project. In this guide, we provide proven templates for every stage of the manufacturing process, from initial inquiry to final shipping.

1. The Initial Inquiry: Getting a Response from Reliable Suppliers

The first email you send to a factory is your "business card." If it is too vague (e.g., "How much for a shirt?"), it will likely be ignored. Factories want to know your volume, your product type, and your level of preparation. Use this template to stand out.

Subject: Inquiry: Custom [Product Category] for [Brand Name] - Tech Pack Attached

Dear [Contact Name/Sales Team],

My name is [Your Name], and I am the founder of [Brand Name], based in [Your Country]. We are looking for a reliable manufacturing partner for our upcoming collection of [specific items, e.g., high-waist yoga leggings].

I have reviewed your profile and believe your factory fits our quality standards. We are looking to produce [Quantity, e.g., 200 units per style].

Could you please let me know:

  • What is your typical MOQ for this type of garment?
  • What is your current lead time for sampling?
  • Do you work with [Specific Fabric, e.g., recycled nylon]?

I have attached a preliminary tech pack for your review. Please let me know if you can assist with this project.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

2. Requesting a Formal Quotation (RFQ)

Once a factory confirms interest, you need a formal quote. This is where you get into the "nitty-gritty" of costs. A Request for Quotation (RFQ) should be specific about materials, quantities, and terms. Remember that price depends heavily on volume, so provide several quantity tiers if possible.

When asking for a quote, ensure you mention "FOB" (Free on Board) or "EXW" (Ex Works) terms so there is no confusion about who pays for local transport and export documents in China. Most experienced buyers prefer FOB prices from large factories.

3. Navigating the Sampling Process via Email

Sampling is the most communicative phase. You will often need to send feedback on prototypes. Use clear, numbered lists to describe changes. Photos with annotations (arrows and text) are far more effective than long paragraphs of text. If a sample is "off," don't just say "it's bad"; say "the inseam is 2cm too short compared to the tech pack."

Always ask for the "Pre-Production Sample" (PPS) before the bulk run begins. This is the final version that represents exactly what the bulk order will look like. Confirming this via email creates a paper trail for quality control later.

4. Managing Production Timelines and Delays

Delays are common in manufacturing due to fabric shortages or holiday schedules (like Chinese New Year). The key is proactive communication. Ask for weekly updates every Tuesday or Wednesday. This keeps your project top-of-mind for the factory manager. If a delay occurs, ask for a revised "T&A" (Time and Action) calendar.

A good template for a check-in: "Hi [Name], could you please provide a status update on order #[123]? Is the fabric currently in the dyeing house or the cutting room? We are planning our marketing launch and need to confirm the estimated ship date."

5. Confirming Shipping and Quality Inspections

Before you pay the final balance, you must confirm that the goods are ready and inspected. Never skip the inspection phase. You can hire a third-party agency (like V-Trust or QIMA) to visit the factory. Email the factory to coordinate their visit. Once the inspection report is "Passed," you can proceed with the final payment and shipping instructions.

Clarify the shipping method: Air Freight (fast, expensive), Sea Freight (slow, cheap), or Rail (a good middle ground for Europe). Ensure the factory provides the Packing List and Commercial Invoice immediately after the goods are packed.
