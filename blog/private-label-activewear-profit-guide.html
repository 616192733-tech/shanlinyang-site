Private Label Activewear Profit Guide: Margins, MOQ & Pricing Strategy

PUBLISHED 2026-07-03 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~15 MIN READ

The activewear industry is one of the most lucrative sectors in global fashion. With the "athleisure" trend showing no signs of slowing down, many entrepreneurs are looking to start their own private label activewear brands. However, turning a passion for fitness into a profitable business requires more than just great designs; it requires a deep understanding of manufacturing costs, pricing strategies, and profit margins.

How much does it actually cost to make a pair of premium leggings? What is a realistic markup? How do MOQs (Minimum Order Quantities) impact your cash flow? These are the questions that define whether a brand thrives or fails. In this guide, we'll pull back the curtain on the business side of activewear manufacturing. At Shanlinyang Apparel, we've helped hundreds of brands scale from their first sample to thousand-piece runs, and we've seen first-hand what makes a brand profitable.

Success in the activewear market is about finding the "sweet spot" between quality and cost. You want to offer a product that competes with Lululemon or Gymshark, but at a price point that allows for sustainable growth. Let's dive into the numbers and strategies behind a profitable private label activewear brand.

1. Understanding Your 'Landed Cost': The True Price of a Garment

When you get a quote from a factory, they will usually give you an FOB (Free On Board) price. This is the price of the garment once it's finished and loaded onto a ship or plane in China. However, this is not your actual cost. To calculate your profit margin, you must understand your "Landed Cost."

Landed Cost includes the unit price plus international shipping, import duties and taxes (which vary significantly by country), insurance, and the cost of final delivery to your warehouse. For many brands, the Landed Cost can be 30-50% higher than the FOB price. For example, a $10 pair of leggings might actually cost you $14.50 by the time they reach your door. If you base your pricing only on the $10 factory price, you are "leaking" profit every time you make a sale.

At Shanlinyang, we help our clients estimate these costs upfront. We can provide DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) quotes for many regions, where we handle the shipping and taxes for you, giving you a single, transparent "all-in" price. This simplifies your accounting and ensures you know your exact margins from day one.

2. The Math of Profit Margins: Target Markups in Activewear

What should your profit margin be? In the premium activewear space, most successful brands aim for a gross margin of at least 60-75%. Gross margin is calculated as (Retail Price - Landed Cost) / Retail Price. If your landed cost is $15, a 70% margin would mean a retail price of $50. If you are selling wholesale to other boutiques, you need to account for their markup as well, usually requiring a 2x-2.5x markup from your cost to the wholesale price.

Why such a high margin? Because after the gross margin, you still have to pay for marketing (which is expensive in the crowded fitness space), website fees, fulfillment, and customer service. High margins give you the "cushion" to invest in influencer marketing and professional photography, which are essential for building a premium brand image. If your margins are too thin, you won't have the budget to acquire new customers.

Consider your "Perceived Value." If your leggings feel and perform like a $90 pair, but you're only charging $40, you might be leaving money on the table. Conversely, if you charge $80 for a product that feels "cheap," your return rate will skyrocket. The quality of manufacturing you get from a partner like Shanlinyang allows you to justify those higher, more profitable price points.

3. The MOQ Strategy: Balancing Risk and Unit Cost

The MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity) is often the biggest hurdle for new brands. Factories have MOQs because setting up a production line—sourcing custom fabric, creating patterns, and adjusting machines—costs the same whether you make 10 pieces or 1,000. Generally, the higher the volume, the lower the unit price. However, ordering 1,000 pieces of a single style as a startup is a massive risk.

A smart strategy is the "Tiered Growth" approach. Start with a manufacturer that offers low MOQs (like Shanlinyang's 100-piece minimum) for custom designs. Yes, your unit cost will be higher, and your margin might only be 50-60% initially. But you are buying "market data." You're seeing which colors and sizes sell fastest. Once you have a "hero product" that you know sells, you can increase your order to 500 or 1,000 pieces, lowering your unit cost and boosting your margin to that 70-75% target.

We also offer "Stock Wholesale" options where MOQs are as low as 2 pieces. This allows brands to "flesh out" their collection with complementary items without a huge capital outlay. For example, you might custom-manufacture your signature leggings but buy stock sports bras to match, keeping your overall investment manageable while offering a full collection.

4. Pricing Strategies: Psychology and Market Positioning

Pricing isn't just a math problem; it's a branding decision. Your price tells the customer where you sit in the market. - **Premium ($80 - $120):** Focuses on technical innovation, exclusivity, and lifestyle. Requires high-end packaging and world-class marketing. - **Mid-Tier ($40 - $70):** Where most private label brands live. Focuses on "attainable quality." Customers expect great performance but are price-conscious. - **Budget ($20 - $35):** Volume-based. Hard for small private labels to compete here against giants like Amazon Essentials or Shein.

Use psychological pricing (e.g., $49 instead of $50). Offer "Bundle and Save" options (e.g., Buy the set and save 15%). Bundling is a great way to increase your Average Order Value (AOV) without significantly increasing your shipping costs, which is a massive win for your net profit. Since our factory can produce matching sets with identical dye lots, you're perfectly positioned to leverage this strategy.

Don't forget to account for "Sale" periods. You should build enough margin into your full-price items that you can still be profitable even during a 20% off Black Friday sale. If your margin is already tight at full price, a sale will put you in the red.

5. Optimizing Your Supply Chain for Maximum Profit

Profit is often found in the efficiencies of your supply chain. - **Consolidated Shipping:** If you're ordering multiple styles, ship them together. Shipping one large crate is much cheaper per unit than five small boxes. - **Fabric Continuity:** Try to use the same fabric for multiple styles. If you use our "80/20 Nylon/Spandex" for leggings, biker shorts, and sports bras, we can buy the fabric in bulk, potentially lowering your unit price across all items. - **Reduce Returns:** In activewear, fit is the #1 reason for returns. Provide detailed size guides and utilize our experience in Western sizing. A 5% reduction in returns can be the difference between profit and loss for the year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What is a typical profit margin for a private label activewear brand?
Successful activewear brands typically aim for a gross margin of 60-75%, meaning if a garment costs $15 to land, it should retail for $40-$60.

Q2: How does MOQ affect my profit margins?
Lower MOQs usually mean higher unit prices. To maximize profit, brands often start small to test designs and then increase order volumes to lower the per-piece cost.

Q3: What are the hidden costs in activewear manufacturing?
Hidden costs include international shipping, import duties/tariffs, transaction fees, third-party inspections, and potential returns/damages.

Q4: Should I choose FOB or DDP shipping for my first order?
For beginners, DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) is easier as the factory handles everything. As you scale, FOB (Free On Board) often allows for better control and lower shipping rates.

Q5: How do I calculate the 'Landed Cost' of my activewear?
Landed Cost = Unit Price + Shipping per piece + Duty per piece + Insurance + Packaging. This is the true cost you should base your retail price on.
