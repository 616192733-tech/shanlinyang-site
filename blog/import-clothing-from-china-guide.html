How to Import Clothing from China: Customs, Duties & Shipping Explained

PUBLISHED 2025-06-27 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~9 MIN READ

Importing clothing from China can seem like a daunting task for many boutique owners and fashion entrepreneurs. Between understanding international shipping terms, navigating customs regulations, and calculating total landed costs, there is a lot to manage. However, with the right knowledge, importing becomes a manageable and highly profitable part of your business operations. This guide breaks down the essential steps to successfully import your garments from China to your warehouse or store.

1. Understanding Incoterms: FOB vs CIF vs DDP

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) define the responsibilities of buyers and sellers in international trade. The three most common terms in clothing manufacturing are:

  • FOB (Free on Board): The factory is responsible for getting the goods to the port of departure (e.g., Xiamen Port) and handling Chinese export customs. You are responsible for the ocean/air freight and all costs from that point forward. This is great for brands with their own freight forwarders.
  • CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight): The seller pays for the freight and insurance to your destination port. However, you are responsible for customs clearance and the "final mile" delivery from the port to your warehouse.
  • DDP (Delivered Duty Paid): This is the most "hands-off" option for the buyer. The seller (or their agent) handles everything—freight, insurance, customs clearance, and all duties/taxes—delivering the goods right to your door. DDP is highly recommended for small to medium-sized brands who don't want to deal with the complexities of customs brokerage.

2. Shipping Methods: Air Freight vs Sea Freight

The choice between air and sea freight depends on your budget, timeline, and order volume.

  • Air Express (DHL/FedEx/UPS): Fastest (3-7 days) and most convenient. Best for samples and small orders (under 100kg). It is "door-to-door" by default.
  • Air Freight: Slower than express (7-12 days) but cheaper for medium-sized shipments (100kg-500kg). Goods go from airport to airport; you'll need a way to move them from the airport to your location.
  • Sea Freight (LCL/FCL): Most cost-effective for large volumes. LCL (Less than Container Load) is for smaller shipments that share a container, while FCL (Full Container Load) is for very large orders. Transit times vary from 20 to 45 days depending on the destination.

3. Navigating Customs Clearance and Paperwork

To get your goods through customs, you need accurate paperwork. The three essential documents are:

  1. Commercial Invoice: Lists the seller, buyer, item description, quantity, unit price, and total value. It must match the payment you sent.
  2. Packing List: Details the contents of each carton, including weights and dimensions.
  3. Bill of Lading (B/L) or Air Waybill (AWB): The contract between the shipper and the carrier.
Errors in these documents can lead to delays or fines. Ensure your factory uses the correct HS Code (Harmonized System Code) for your garments, as this determines the duty rate. For example, knitted dresses and woven trousers have different HS codes and different tax rates.

4. Hidden Costs: Port Fees, Insurance, and Brokerage

When calculating your "landed cost" (the total cost of a product once it reaches your door), don't forget the smaller fees. If you aren't using DDP, you will likely encounter:

  • Customs Brokerage Fees: The fee paid to a professional to handle your customs entry.
  • Port Handling Charges: Fees for moving containers at the port.
  • Insurance: Usually around 0.3% to 0.5% of the cargo value. It is vital protection against loss or damage during transit.
  • Demurrage/Detention: Fines if you leave your goods at the port or with the shipping line for too long.
Always ask your freight forwarder for a "all-in" quote to avoid surprises at the end of the journey.

5. Best Practices for Smooth International Delivery

To ensure your shipment arrives without a hitch, follow these tips:

  • Use a Reliable Freight Forwarder: A good forwarder is worth their weight in gold. They can consolidate shipments, find the best routes, and solve problems as they arise.
  • Verify Labeling Requirements: Different countries have different labeling laws (e.g., country of origin, fabric content, care instructions). Ensure your garments are labeled correctly at the factory.
  • Track Your Shipments: Use the tracking numbers provided by your carrier or forwarder to monitor progress.
  • Plan for Delays: Especially during peak seasons (like Chinese New Year or the pre-Christmas rush), shipping can take longer than expected. Build a buffer into your launch schedule.

Importing from China is a skill that improves with experience. By mastering these logistics, you can significantly lower your costs and increase your brand's competitiveness in the global market.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the fastest way to ship clothing from China?

Air express (like DHL, FedEx, or UPS) is the fastest method, usually taking 3-7 days. Air freight is slightly slower but more cost-effective for larger shipments.

How much are import duties on clothing from China?

Import duties vary by country and the type of garment. In the US, they typically range from 10% to 32%, plus additional Section 301 tariffs.

What documents do I need for customs clearance?

The essential documents are the Commercial Invoice, Packing List, and Bill of Lading (or Air Waybill). You may also need a Certificate of Origin.

What does DDP shipping mean?

Delivered Duty Paid (DDP) means the seller handles all shipping costs, duties, and taxes until the goods arrive at your door. It is the most convenient option for many small brands.

Can I ship directly to an Amazon FBA warehouse from China?

Yes, many factories and freight forwarders offer direct shipping to Amazon FBA warehouses, including labeling and palletization services.
