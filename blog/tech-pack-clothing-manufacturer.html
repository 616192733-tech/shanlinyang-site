What Is a Tech Pack? How to Create One for Your Clothing Manufacturer

PUBLISHED 2026-06-29 · SHANLINYANG APPAREL FACTORY TEAM · ~12 MIN READ

In the world of fashion manufacturing, the transition from a creative idea to a physical garment is fraught with potential misunderstandings. A common question for new brand owners is: "How do I ensure the factory makes exactly what I envisioned?" The answer lies in a single, critical document: the **Tech Pack**. A tech pack (technical packet) is the blueprint for your garment, providing every detail your **tech pack clothing manufacturer** needs to produce your design accurately and efficiently.

Without a professional tech pack, you are essentially asking your manufacturer to guess your preferences. This leads to costly sampling rounds, wasted fabric, and delays in your launch. At Shanlinyang Apparel, we have seen first-hand how a well-constructed tech pack can cut development time by 50% and reduce errors significantly. In this guide, we will break down the essential components of a tech pack and provide a roadmap for creating your own.

1. Why Your Tech Pack Is the Most Important Document in Fashion Manufacturing

Think of a tech pack as a contract between you and your manufacturer. It defines the standards by which the finished product will be judged. For a tech pack clothing manufacturer, this document is the primary reference used by pattern makers, sewers, and quality control inspectors. Its importance cannot be overstated for several reasons:

Accuracy and Consistency: A tech pack ensures that every piece in a 1,000-unit production run is identical. It removes ambiguity regarding measurements, seam types, and finishing details. If you want a specific 1cm topstitch on the collar, the tech pack is where that requirement is legally and technically recorded.

Cost Estimation: Manufacturers use the "Bill of Materials" (BOM) within the tech pack to calculate the cost of goods. By specifying the exact fabric weight, button type, and thread count, you allow the factory to provide a precise quote rather than a rough estimate. This helps you manage your profit margins from day one.

Accountability: If a production batch arrives and the pockets are in the wrong place or the fabric is the wrong color, the tech pack serves as the baseline for resolution. If the error contradicts the tech pack, the manufacturer is responsible. If the tech pack was missing that information, the responsibility falls on the brand. This clarity protects both parties.

Speed to Market: Comprehensive tech packs allow factories to get it right the first time. Instead of three or four sampling rounds to "find the fit," a good tech pack can lead to a near-perfect proto-sample. In the fast-paced fashion industry, saving two weeks in development can be the difference between hitting a trend or missing it entirely.

2. Essential Components of a Professional Tech Pack for Manufacturers

A professional tech pack is not just a single page; it is a multi-page document that covers different aspects of the garment's construction. Here are the non-negotiable sections every tech pack clothing manufacturer expects to see:

Technical Sketches (Flats): These are 2D, black-and-white drawings of the garment, showing both front and back views. They should include call-outs for specific features like zippers, pockets, and stitching details. Unlike fashion illustrations, flats are drawn to scale and avoid artistic flourishes.

Bill of Materials (BOM): This is a comprehensive list of every physical component that goes into the garment. It includes the primary fabric (shell), lining, interlining, threads, buttons, zippers, labels, and even the polybags used for packaging. For each item, you should specify the supplier (if known), color code, and material composition.

Measurement Specification (Spec Sheet): This is perhaps the most technical part. It lists all key measurements (Points of Measure or POM) for every size you intend to produce. For a t-shirt, this includes chest width, body length, sleeve length, neck opening, and more. It also includes the "tolerance"—the acceptable deviation (usually +/- 0.5cm to 1cm) for each measurement.

Construction Details: This section specifies how the garment is put together. It should include stitch types (e.g., overlock, flatlock, single-needle topstitch), stitches per inch (SPI), and seam allowances. Using diagrams to show how complex areas like waistbands or plackets are constructed is highly recommended.

Branding and Artwork: If your garment features a screen print, embroidery, or custom labels, this section provides the artwork files, placement coordinates, and color references (usually Pantone codes). High-resolution vector files (.AI or .EPS) are mandatory for this section.

3. Step-by-Step Guide: How to Create a Tech Pack from Scratch

Creating a tech pack can seem daunting, but it follows a logical flow. Here is a step-by-step approach to building a document that will satisfy any professional tech pack clothing manufacturer:

Step 1: Define the Concept and Style Number: Assign a unique style number (e.g., SS26-001) to your design. This makes it easy to track in the factory's system. Briefly describe the garment's purpose (e.g., "High-Waist Compression Leggings").

Step 2: Create Technical Flats: Use Adobe Illustrator to draw your flats. Ensure you include a front view, back view, and any necessary "detail views" (like a close-up of a hidden pocket). Use clear, thin lines and avoid shading.

Step 3: Build the BOM: List every trim and fabric. Be specific. Instead of "Black Button," write "20L Matte Black Urea 4-Hole Button." If you don't have the exact specs yet, provide reference photos of the look you want.

Step 4: Establish Your Grade Rules: Decide on your size range (e.g., XS to XL). Create a measurement spec for your "base size" (usually Medium). Then, define how each measurement changes for the larger and smaller sizes. This process is called "grading."

Step 5: Review and Refine: Walk through the garment's construction in your head. Did you include the care label? Is the thread color specified? Once you are satisfied, export the document as a single PDF. Ensure the file size is manageable for email but clear enough for printing.

4. Common Tech Pack Mistakes That Lead to Production Errors

Even experienced designers can make mistakes that confuse their tech pack clothing manufacturer. Here are the most common pitfalls to avoid:

Vague Measurements: Using terms like "regular fit" or "true to size" is useless in a tech pack. Everything must be quantified in centimeters or inches. If you want a loose fit, define the chest measurement as significantly wider than the body's actual measurement.

Missing the "Inside": Many beginners forget to show how the inside of the garment looks. For jackets or lined dresses, the interior construction is just as important as the exterior. Show the lining, internal pockets, and seam finishes (like Hong Kong seams).

Inconsistent Terminology: Using different names for the same part (e.g., "neckband" on one page and "collar" on another) can lead to confusion on the factory floor. Stick to industry-standard terminology throughout the document.

Ignoring Fabric Properties: A tech pack for a woven silk dress will look very different from a tech pack for a high-stretch nylon legging. You must account for "shrinkage" and "stretch" when defining measurements. If you aren't sure, ask your manufacturer for advice on how a particular fabric behaves.

5. Scaling Your Brand: Managing Multiple Tech Packs with Chinese Suppliers

As your brand grows from a single style to a full collection, managing tech packs becomes a logistical challenge. Working with a tech pack clothing manufacturer in China requires a system for version control.

Version Control is Key: Every time you make a change to a tech pack after sampling, update the "Version Number" and date (e.g., V1.1, V2.0). Never let a manufacturer work off an old PDF. Always ensure the latest version is the only one in circulation.

Standardize Your Templates: Using a consistent format for all your tech packs makes it easier for the factory's team to find information quickly. Whether you use Excel or a PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) tool, keep the structure identical across styles.

Digital Libraries: Maintain a cloud-based library of your fabrics and trims. Once you have approved a particular button or fabric from a manufacturer, you can simply reference its "Item Code" in future tech packs, saving time and ensuring consistency across seasons.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What is a tech pack in clothing manufacturing?
A tech pack is a comprehensive document that serves as a blueprint for your garment. It includes technical drawings, measurements, fabric specifications, bill of materials, and assembly instructions for your clothing manufacturer.

Q2: Do I need a tech pack for every design?
Yes, a tech pack is essential for every unique style. It ensures accuracy, consistency across production runs, and serves as a legal reference for quality control.

Q3: Can I create a tech pack myself?
While brand owners can draft initial versions, professional tech packs are usually created by technical designers to ensure all manufacturing requirements are met correctly.

Q4: What software is used for tech packs?
Common tools include Adobe Illustrator for technical sketches, Excel or specialized PLM software like Techpacker or Backbone for data management.
