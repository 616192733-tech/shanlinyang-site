GUIDE — FINANCIAL SECURITY

Payment Terms with Chinese Clothing Manufacturers: T/T, Trade Assurance & More

Understanding financial transactions is a critical part of international sourcing. When manufacturing in China, having clear, secure payment terms protects both the brand owner and the factory.

For many new clothing brand founders, the process of sending thousands of dollars overseas can be intimidating. However, the Chinese manufacturing industry has established standard practices that minimize risk for both parties. In this guide, we’ll explain the most common clothing manufacturer payment terms China and how to choose the right method for your business stage.

1. The Standard 30/70 Payment Split

The most widely accepted payment structure in the garment industry is the **30% deposit / 70% balance** split. Here is how it works:

  • 30% Deposit: Paid upfront to secure production space and purchase raw materials (fabric, trims, etc.). Production only begins after the factory receives this payment.
  • 70% Balance: Paid once production is finished. Crucially, this should only be paid *after* you have received a QC inspection report (either from the factory or a third party) and are satisfied with the results.

This split provides the factory with the capital needed to start work while giving the buyer leverage to ensure quality before the final payment is made.

2. T/T (Telegraphic Transfer): The B2B Standard

T/T is simply a bank-to-bank international wire transfer. It is the preferred method for most Chinese factories because it is direct and has relatively low fees compared to other methods.

When paying via T/T, always ensure you are sending money to the factory’s registered business account, not a personal account. Most factories will provide a "Proforma Invoice" (PI) containing their official bank details, including their SWIFT code and IBAN.

3. Alibaba Trade Assurance: Protection for New Brands

If you find your manufacturer through Alibaba, "Trade Assurance" is an excellent tool. It acts as an escrow service. You pay Alibaba, and they hold the funds until you confirm that the goods have been received and meet the agreed-upon quality standards.

While Alibaba charges a transaction fee (usually around 2-3%), the peace of mind it provides for a first-time order with a new factory is often worth the cost. At Shanlinyang, we frequently use Trade Assurance for first-time clients to build a foundation of trust.

4. PayPal and Credit Cards: Best for Small Amounts

PayPal and Credit Cards are commonly used for sampling fees or very small test orders. They offer excellent buyer protection and are instant. However, the transaction fees (often 4-5%) and less favorable exchange rates make them expensive for large bulk production orders. Most factories will ask the buyer to cover the PayPal transaction fee.

5. Managing Currency and Exchange Rates

Most Chinese manufacturers quote in US Dollars (USD). If your business operates in another currency (like EUR or GBP), keep an eye on exchange rate fluctuations, as they can affect your final landed cost. Some larger brands use currency hedging, but for most small brands, simply building a small buffer into your budget is sufficient.

6. Tips for a Smooth Financial Relationship

  • Always use a Proforma Invoice (PI): Ensure it details the style numbers, quantities, unit prices, and lead times.
  • Request a Proof of Payment: After sending a wire transfer, send a PDF of the bank receipt to the factory. This helps their accounting team track the funds and start production faster.
  • Be wary of "sudden" bank changes: If a factory asks you to send money to a new, different bank account at the last minute, call them to verify. This is a common tactic for email hackers.
