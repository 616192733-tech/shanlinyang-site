Clothing Manufacturer Deposit & Payment Guide: How to Pay Safely in China

Managing financial transactions with overseas suppliers is one of the most critical aspects of sourcing. Ensuring your money is safe and your production is secured requires a clear understanding of Chinese business practices and secure payment methods. This guide covers everything you need to know about paying your clothing manufacturer safely.

For many new fashion brand owners, sending thousands of dollars to a factory thousands of miles away can be nerve-wracking. Understanding the clothing manufacturer deposit payment China standards will help alleviate this anxiety and protect your business from unnecessary risks.

Safe payment isn't just about the method you use; it's about the timing and the documentation that supports the transaction. Here is our expert guide to financial security in the apparel supply chain.

1. Standard Payment Terms in the Chinese Apparel Industry (30/70)

In the vast majority of cases, you will encounter the "30/70" payment term. This means:

  • 30% Deposit: Paid upfront to confirm the order. The factory uses this to purchase raw materials (fabrics, trims) and schedule the production line.
  • 70% Balance: Paid after the goods are finished and have passed your final quality inspection, but before they are loaded onto the ship or plane.

This structure protects both parties. The factory has the capital to start work, and you retain 70% of the funds as leverage to ensure the quality is correct. Never agree to pay more than 50% as a deposit for bulk production unless you have a long-standing relationship with the supplier.

2. Secure Payment Methods: T/T, Letter of Credit, and Trade Assurance

There are several ways to send money to China, each with its own pros and cons:

  • Telegraphic Transfer (T/T): The most common method. It's fast and accepted by all factories. To stay safe, ensure the bank account name matches the "Company Name" on their business license. Avoid paying into personal bank accounts.
  • Alibaba Trade Assurance: Excellent for new relationships. It acts as an escrow service, holding your funds until you confirm receipt or the inspection is passed.
  • Letter of Credit (L/C): Generally used for very large orders (above $50,000). It's a bank-guaranteed payment that is only released when specific shipping documents are presented. It's very secure but involves high bank fees.
  • PayPal: Good for small sampling fees, but rarely used for bulk production due to high transaction fees (around 4-5%).

3. Why You Should Never Pay 100% Upfront

It might be tempting to pay 100% upfront to "get it over with" or to save on transfer fees. **Do not do this.**

Paying 100% upfront removes any incentive for the factory to prioritize your order or to fix quality issues found during inspection. If the factory encounters a delay or a material shortage, they will prioritize the clients who still owe them money. Your 70% balance is your insurance policy. Keep it until you are certain the goods are exactly what you ordered.

4. Dealing with Currency Fluctuations and Transaction Fees

Most Chinese manufacturers quote in US Dollars (USD). However, their costs (labor, local materials) are in Chinese Yuan (RMB). If the RMB strengthens significantly against the USD, the factory may ask for a price adjustment for future orders.

When sending money, be aware of the "intermediary bank fees." If you send exactly $10,000, the factory might only receive $9,970 because banks along the way take a cut. To maintain a good relationship, either add a small buffer for these fees or use a service like Wise (formerly TransferWise) which shows the exact amount the recipient will receive.

5. Red Flags: When a Payment Request Doesn't Feel Right

Trust your instincts. If any of the following happen, stop and investigate before sending money:

  • Sudden Bank Account Change: If the factory tells you at the last minute to send money to a different account (especially one in a different country like Hong Kong or Singapore), verify this via a phone call to a known contact at the factory. This is a common email hacking scam.
  • Pressure to pay 100%: If they claim they need the full amount for "urgent material costs," it may indicate the factory is in financial trouble.
  • Payment to a Personal Account: Professional factories use corporate accounts. Paying an individual offers zero legal protection.
